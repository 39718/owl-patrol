About the Project

Owl Patrol — an overview of the initiative and its objectives.

Owl Patrol is a neighborhood-based initiative focused on the construction and installation of nesting boxes designed specifically for the barn owl (Tyto alba). The objective is to provide barn owl families with suitable habitat within residential areas, enabling them to perform their role as natural predators of rodents without the use of chemical intervention.

This project was developed in response to the widespread use of anticoagulant rodenticides in residential settings. These substances are harmful not only to the rodents they target, but to the broader food chain — including raptors, foxes, and domestic animals that consume poisoned prey. Owl Patrol offers a measurable, ecologically sound alternative.

Project Benefits

What this project does for the community.

🦉

Habitat and Food Source for Owls

Natural nesting sites for barn owls — including old barns and hollow trees — are increasingly scarce in residential environments. Installed nest boxes provide a stable habitat for breeding pairs, while the surrounding neighborhood offers an adequate prey base.

🌿

Support for the Local Ecosystem

The reintroduction of barn owls as active predators within a neighborhood contributes to ecological balance. As apex predators of small mammals, their presence has measurable effects on the broader local food web.

☠️

Reduction in Rodenticide Use

Anticoagulant rodenticides are among the most ecologically damaging substances in common residential use. The presence of established barn owl families reduces dependence on these compounds as a means of rodent population management.

🦅

Prevention of Secondary Poisoning

Rodenticides do not remain contained to their intended targets. Predatory animals — including owls, hawks, foxes, and coyotes — that consume poisoned rodents are subject to secondary poisoning. Eliminating rodenticide use interrupts this chain of harm.

🐾

Reduced Risk to Domestic Animals

Domestic pets are vulnerable to rodenticide exposure through direct contact or the consumption of affected rodents. A neighborhood in which natural predation replaces chemical control presents a substantially lower risk to household animals.

💡

Low Cost and Low Maintenance

Once a nest box is constructed and installed, ongoing maintenance requirements are minimal. Barn owl pairs are site-faithful and will return to the same box each season, providing continuous, self-sustaining rodent control at no recurring cost.

How It Works

1

Box Construction

Boxes are crafted with the precise interior dimensions required to accommodate a barn owl family, using untreated wood that does not introduce harmful substances into the nesting environment.

2

Box Installation

Each box is mounted at an appropriate height on a pole or tree, oriented to minimize exposure to prevailing winds and direct sunlight, in accordance with established barn owl nesting guidelines.

3

Owls Nest

Barn owls independently scout and select suitable nesting locations. A correctly sited box positioned within suitable foraging range will typically attract a nesting pair within the first season.

4

Hunting Begins

Once a pair has taken up residence, nightly foraging of the surrounding area commences. The pair raises successive clutches within the box and returns to the same site in subsequent seasons.

By the Numbers

What makes barn owls so effective.

  • A single barn owl family consumes a minimum of 3,000 rodents per year, accounting for both the adult pair and their offspring during the nesting season.

  • Barn owls are capable of hunting in near-total darkness, locating prey exclusively by sound through the use of asymmetrical ear placement and a parabolic facial disc.

  • A single clutch typically consists of 4 to 7 owlets, each of which requires multiple rodents per night to support development through the fledging period.

  • Barn owl pairs are monogamous and highly site-faithful. Once a nesting box is occupied, the pair will return to the same location in each subsequent breeding season.

  • A correctly sited nest box is capable of attracting an occupying pair within the first season following installation.

About the Project Coordinator

About Me

My name is Bilge, and I am 13 years old. Owl Patrol began as an extension of work I was doing in science class, where we were studying owls as a means of understanding broader ecological relationships.

During that time, I observed that our yard had a significant number of rodent burrows despite the presence of bait boxes — a chemical approach that was clearly not resolving the problem. Drawing on what I had learned about barn owls and their role as natural predators, I decided to apply that knowledge practically rather than leave it in the classroom.

Owl Patrol is the result of that decision. The project has been in development for several weeks and remains ongoing. My aim is to demonstrate that a sustainable, ecologically informed approach to rodent management is not only viable, but preferable — even at a neighborhood scale.
